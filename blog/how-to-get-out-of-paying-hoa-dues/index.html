How to Handle HOA Dues When Selling in VA

Dealing with HOA Dues When Selling Your Virginia Home

Homeowners association fees are a reality of life for many Hampton Roads homeowners. Whether you live in a condo, townhouse, or single-family home within a planned community, HOA dues are a recurring expense that can become burdensome, especially when combined with a mortgage, property taxes, and insurance costs.

If you are looking to sell your home and want to understand your options regarding HOA obligations, this guide covers what you need to know as a Virginia homeowner.

Understanding HOA Obligations in Virginia

In Virginia, HOA fees are a legal obligation tied to property ownership within a community governed by a homeowners association. The Virginia Property Owners Association Act and the Virginia Condominium Act establish the legal framework for HOA operations in the state.

HOA dues typically cover common area maintenance, landscaping, community amenities, insurance for shared structures, and reserve funds for future repairs. Monthly fees in Hampton Roads range from 50 dollars for basic neighborhood associations to 400 dollars or more for waterfront condo communities with extensive amenities.

What Happens If You Fall Behind on HOA Dues

Unpaid HOA dues in Virginia can result in serious consequences. The HOA can place a lien on your property, charge late fees and interest, restrict your access to community amenities, and in extreme cases pursue foreclosure. Virginia law gives HOAs the power to foreclose on properties with unpaid assessments, even if your mortgage payments are current.

If you have fallen behind on HOA payments, the balance plus any fees and interest must typically be paid at closing when you sell your home. The title company will request a payoff statement from the HOA and ensure all outstanding amounts are satisfied from the sale proceeds.

Options for Homeowners Struggling with HOA Fees

Negotiate a Payment Plan

Many HOAs will work with homeowners who are behind on dues to establish a payment plan. Contact your HOA board or management company to discuss your situation. Getting ahead of the problem is always better than waiting for collection actions to begin.

Request a Hardship Waiver

Some HOAs have provisions for temporary hardship waivers or fee reductions for homeowners experiencing financial difficulties. Review your community's governing documents or ask the board about any available relief programs.

Sell Your Home

If HOA fees are contributing to an unsustainable financial situation, selling your home may be the most practical solution. Outstanding HOA dues and liens are paid off from the sale proceeds at closing. A cash buyer can close quickly, stopping the accumulation of additional fees and interest, and allowing you to move to a home without HOA obligations if you prefer.

HOA Considerations When Selling in Hampton Roads

When selling a home in an HOA community in Virginia, the seller is required to provide the buyer with a resale disclosure packet. This packet includes the community's governing documents, financial statements, and information about any pending assessments or litigation. Your HOA or its management company typically prepares this packet for a fee ranging from 100 to 400 dollars.

Be transparent about any outstanding HOA issues when selling your home. Buyers and their lenders will discover any liens or unpaid assessments during the title search, so addressing these proactively helps ensure a smooth closing process.
